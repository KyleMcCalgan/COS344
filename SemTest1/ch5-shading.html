Chapters 4.5 + 5

Surface Shading

How to compute the colour of a surface when a ray (or fragment) hits it. Covers light sources, Lambertian diffuse, Blinn-Phong specular, ambient illumination, and the BRDF framework.

Connects to → Ch 4: Shading called after ray intersection Ch 9: Gouraud & Phong shading in pipeline Ch 3: Output as RGB pixel colour

What is Shading? § overview

Shading computes the colour of a surface point based on:

  • The surface normal at the hit point
  • The light source(s) in the scene
  • The material properties of the surface
  • The viewing direction
📐
Shading model

Equations used to compute shading. Shading models are independent of the rendering system — the same model can be used in both ray tracing and rasterization.

Four key vectors

x — shading point
x = e + t·d
Where the ray intersects the surface (computed in Ch 4)
n — surface normal
n = normal at x
Unit vector perpendicular to the surface at the hit point
l — light direction
l = normalize(p − x)
Points FROM the surface TOWARD the light. Different for point vs directional.
v — view direction
v = −d / |d|
Opposite of the ray direction — points from surface toward the eye

Section 4.5 / 5.1 — Light Sources

Light TypeDescriptionl directionExample
Point light Illuminates from a single point in space. Illuminates surfaces differently depending on position/distance. normalize(p − x) where p = light pos Light bulb, torch
Directional light Very far away — all rays are parallel. No position, only direction. Constant irradiance H. Constant direction vector (specified) Sun
Ambient light Global constant illumination filling in shadows. No direction, no position. N/A — independent of geometry Sky glow, indirect bounce

Irradiance for a point light source

E = I · (n · l) / r² Where: I = intensity of the light source (I = P / 4π) r = distance from light to surface point n = surface normal (unit vector) l = direction toward light (unit vector) n·l = cos θ (Lambert's cosine law) Simplified: E = I · max(0, n·l) / r²

Directional light irradiance

E = H · cos θ = H · (n · l) Where H = constant normal irradiance (replaces I/r² since r → ∞)

Section 5.1.1 — BRDF Framework

The BRDF (Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function) defines how light from a source is reflected off an opaque surface toward a viewer.

L_r = k · E Where: L_r = reflected radiance (what the viewer sees) k = material BRDF coefficient (varies by model) E = irradiance at the surface point
💡
Software split: Light vs Material

In practice: the Light object handles overall illumination (computing E), and the Material object handles BRDF computation (k). This separation makes shading models swappable.

Algorithm — Point Light Illumination (pseudo)
function shade_point(lightPos p, surfacePoint x)
r = |p − x|
l = (p − x) / r
n = surfaceNormal(x)
E = max(0, n·l) · I / r²
k = BRDF(material, l, v, n)
return k · E
end function

Section 5.2.1 — Lambertian (Diffuse) Reflection

An ideal diffuse surface (Lambertian surface) reflects light equally in all directions regardless of where the light comes from.

✅
Properties of Lambertian surfaces

Brightness is the same from all viewing angles. Colour is independent of viewing direction. Described by a single reflectance value R (RGB). Gives a flat, chalky appearance — good for paper, flat paint, dirt.

L_r = (R / π) · E For point light, expanded: L_r = (R / π) · max(0, n·l) · I / r² Where R = surface reflectance (RGB colour of the material)

Section 5.2.2 — Blinn-Phong Specular Reflection

Specular reflection is view-dependent — the shiny highlight moves as you move the camera. The Blinn-Phong model uses a half vector h.

Half vector: h = normalize(l + v) Specular component: (n · h)^p Where p = Phong exponent (shininess) Large p → tight, small highlight (very shiny) Small p → broad, soft highlight (rough surface)

Combined Lambertian + Blinn-Phong

L_r = [ R/π + k_s · max(0, n·h)^p ] · E Where: R = diffuse reflectance (RGB) k_s = specular coefficient (RGB) p = Phong exponent (shininess) h = half vector = normalize(l + v) E = irradiance from light source
🎯
Exam tip — half vector

Be able to compute the half vector h = normalize(l + v) and explain why it's used: when h aligns with the surface normal n, the surface is at maximum specularity (mirror-like reflection for that angle).

Mirror reflection (§4.5.4)

Reflection direction: r = d − 2(d·n)·n Color contribution: c += k_m · shade(reflected_ray) Where k_m = mirror reflection coefficient (specular RGB color)

Section 5.3 — Ambient Illumination

Ambient light is a constant that brightens all surfaces equally, simulating indirect/bounce lighting to prevent unlit areas from being completely black.

L_r = k_a · I_a Where: k_a = ambient reflection coefficient (material property) I_a = ambient intensity (scene-wide constant) Note: No n, l, or v needed — direction-independent.

Full combined shading model

L_total = k_a·I_a // Ambient + Σ_lights [ (R/π + k_s·(n·h)^p)·E_i ] // Diffuse + Specular per light + k_m · shade(reflected_ray) // Mirror reflection (optional)
